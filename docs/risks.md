**What's actually strong**

The "extraction before interpretation" principle is exactly right and rare. Most journaling/memory products invert this. Your ethical guardrails around romanticization are unusually rigorous. The fallback prompt system (sensory anchors, relationship anchors, micro-moments) is practically brilliant — you've solved the blank page problem before it becomes a product failure.

---

**The blind spots I'd push on**

**1. The emotional labor problem — your biggest risk**

This process will surface grief, regret, trauma, and estrangement. Regularly. Not as edge cases — as a near-certainty for anyone over 50 with a full life. Your documents correctly say "not therapy," but the product *will feel like therapy* to many users, and some sessions will open wounds without any trained human to help close them.

You need a concrete plan for this — not a disclaimer, but an actual design response. What does the AI do when someone starts crying mid-session about a lost child? What does "not therapy" mean in practice at that moment? This gap could become a legal, ethical, and reputational liability.

**2. The audience mismatch between your vision and your UX**

Your primary wedge — elderly people wanting to preserve stories for family — and your UX assumptions are in tension. Voice-first is smart. But "Facebook API hooks," "chapter-based release," "manga/anime adaptations" — these features will confuse or alienate the 70-year-old with early Alzheimer's who is arguably your most emotionally resonant and word-of-mouth-powerful user. You may be building for two very different people simultaneously without acknowledging that tension.

Who is the *first* user? Design for them completely. Add the others later.

**3. The "family wants it" vs. "subject wants it" conflict**

Your documents frame this almost entirely from the subject's perspective. But in practice, a significant portion of your users will be *adult children* initiating this for a parent who is reluctant, passive, or cognitively declining. The consent and agency dynamics here are genuinely complex. Who owns the archive? Who can access it? What if the subject says something in session they'd never want their children to read? This needs explicit thinking — not just in privacy policy, but in product design.

**4. The "done" problem is deeper than you've noted**

You flagged "when is a biography done?" as a strategic question, but the deeper problem is: what happens when the subject dies mid-process? This is not a hypothetical — it's a near-certainty for your elderly segment. You need an inheritance/custody protocol designed *in advance*, not as an afterthought. Who gets the Codex? Can it be queried after death? This is where "not immortality" meets a very concrete product decision.

**5. Your positioning risks sounding cold to non-technical buyers**

Language like "systematically extract," "structured memory extraction," "the system always has a structured next question" — this is excellent engineering thinking, but if it leaks into marketing or onboarding, it will repel the exact audience you want. People don't want their grandmother's memories "extracted." They want them *heard*. The howto-biography.md document is a great internal spec; it should never be customer-facing in that register.

---

**A blind spot specific to your Asperger's note**

The warmth and relational texture of the interviewing experience will be the product's actual moat — more than the data structure. Users won't stay because the Codex is well-indexed. They'll stay because the AI made them feel genuinely listened to. This is the hardest thing to build and test analytically, and it's worth finding a collaborator or tester specifically to pressure-test the *felt experience* of sessions, not just the architecture. Consider recruiting 3–5 people in your target demographic and watching them use it live — not reading their feedback, watching them.

---

**One strategic question I'd add to your list**

You mention "ego-driven high achievers" as a segment. This is actually your easiest early monetization path — they'll pay more, they're easier to reach, and they have clearer ROI framing ("my memoirs, without hiring a ghostwriter"). But they're philosophically at odds with your vision document's soul. **Decision: secondary segment only.** The primary design focus stays on the elderly/family use case. High-achievers may be served later if the product naturally accommodates them, but every core design decision (pacing, emotional care, voice-first UX, family access controls) is built for the primary segment first.

---

The foundation is genuinely solid. The risks above aren't reasons to doubt the project — they're the specific gaps worth closing before you build too far in one direction.
